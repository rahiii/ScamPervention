Phishing & Social Engineering Scams

Understanding the Threat: Cybercriminals employ sophisticated techniques to manipulate you into revealing personal information, financial details, or login credentials. By knowing how these scams work—from urgent phishing emails to deceptive texts and calls—you can protect yourself effectively.

Phishing (Email Scams)

Description: Phishing scams use deceptive emails that impersonate trusted institutions such as banks, online services, or employers. These emails may warn you of account suspension, unauthorized access, or security breaches—and include harmful attachments that install malware when opened.

Common Tactics in Phishing

  • Fake Urgency: Example: “Your account has been locked due to unusual activity! Log in now to restore access.” (The link directs you to a counterfeit login page.)
  • Impersonation: Example: An email appearing to be from Amazon stating, “Your recent order could not be processed. Click here to update your payment information.” (Clicking the link leads to a fraudulent payment page.)
  • Malicious Attachments: Example: An email from a company’s HR with an attachment titled “Updated Benefits Package.” (Opening it installs malware.)

How to Spot a Phishing Scam

  • Emails that demand immediate action over security issues.
  • Sender addresses or domains that are slightly off from the authentic ones.
  • Unexpected attachments from unknown senders.
  • Generic greetings like “Dear Customer” instead of your name.

Smishing (SMS Scams)

Description: Smishing uses fraudulent text messages that appear to come from reputable companies (such as banks or shipping services). These messages include links to fake login pages or malware downloads. Since SMS messages lack visual security cues like verified sender domains, extra caution is essential.

Common Tactics in Smishing

  • Delivery Scams: Example: “Your FedEx package is delayed. Click here to reschedule: [bit.ly/fake-link].”
  • Banking Alerts: Example: “Suspicious login detected! Verify your identity immediately: [fake-bank-link].”
  • Fake Prize Notifications: Example: “Congratulations! You’ve won a $500 Walmart gift card. Claim it now: [fraudulent-link].”

How to Spot a Smishing Scam

  • Texts from unknown numbers urging immediate action.
  • Messages containing shortened or suspicious links.
  • Requests for personal or financial information via SMS.
  • Unexpected alerts about packages, bank issues, or prizes.

Vishing (Phone Call Scams)

Description: In vishing scams, fraudsters call you while posing as representatives of banks, government agencies, or tech support. These calls create urgency—claiming account compromises, tax issues, or computer infections—in order to pressure you into revealing sensitive details.

Common Tactics in Vishing

  • Caller ID Spoofing: Example: A caller appears to be from your bank, warns you of a compromise, and requests your login credentials.
  • Fear Tactics: Example: An impersonated IRS agent claims you owe thousands and threatens legal action unless you pay immediately.
  • Tech Support Scams: Example: A pop-up warns, “Your system has been infected! Call Apple Support now.” (You are urged to download remote access software.)

How to Spot a Vishing Scam

  • Unexpected calls from “support teams” asking for remote access or sensitive data.
  • Requests for payment via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers.
  • Calls that use urgency or threats of legal consequences.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Unsolicited messages asking for personal details.
  • Urgent language promising severe consequences.
  • Links that lead to fake login pages (hover to inspect URLs).
  • Unexpected attachments and low-quality images or logos.
  • Calls demanding immediate payment—especially via gift cards or cryptocurrency.
  • Offers that seem too good to be true.

If You Click a Suspicious Link

  1. Change your password immediately: Use a strong, unique combination of letters, numbers, and symbols.
  2. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Add an extra layer of security to your accounts.
  3. Run a full antivirus scan: Ensure your device is free from malware that might have been installed.
  4. Report the phishing message: Forward it to your provider or the authorities (for example, reportphishing@apwg.org) and file a complaint with the FTC at ftc.gov/Complaint.
