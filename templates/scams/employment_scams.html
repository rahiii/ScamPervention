Job, Internship, and Scholarship Scams

Understanding the Threat: Scammers target students, recent graduates, and job seekers by posing as legitimate employers, recruiters, or scholarship committees. These scams are often delivered via email, job boards, social media platforms like LinkedIn or Instagram, or even through campus mailing lists. Victims may be promised high-paying remote roles, internship opportunities, or financial awards—only to be tricked into sending personal data, paying upfront fees, or unknowingly laundering money.

Common Tactics in Job, Internship, and Scholarship Scams

  • Too-Good-to-Be-True Job Offers: Example: “We’d love to offer you a fully remote job paying $1,200 a week. No interview needed!” (Scammers often promote jobs with high pay, minimal effort, and instant hiring to lure victims.)
  • Fake Scholarship or Membership Invitations: Example: “You’ve been selected for the Honor Society! Just pay a $75 activation fee to access your benefits.” (These scams ask for fees before awarding anything and use flattery or urgency to pressure victims.)
  • Phishing Links in Application Emails: Example: A recruiter on LinkedIn asks you to click a shortened link to view a “confidential proposal.” (These links often lead to phishing sites or malware.)
  • Externship Confirmation Scams: Example: “You’ve been accepted to our externship program! Click here to confirm within 48 hours or the offer will be revoked.” (Scammers use fake urgency and prohibit contacting the real company to avoid being exposed.)
  • Check Deposit Scams: Example: “As part of your job, deposit this check and wire part of the money to a vendor.” (The check bounces, leaving you responsible for the money sent.)

How to Spot a Job, Internship, or Scholarship Scam

  • Offers that appear without applying or interviewing.
  • Requests for payment or processing fees before receiving anything.
  • Communications that use urgency: “respond now,” “last chance,” “limited slots.”
  • Unprofessional email addresses (e.g., @gmail.com) instead of official domains.
  • Recruiters who avoid video calls or won’t share verifiable company information.
  • Links that redirect to unfamiliar or suspicious-looking websites.
  • Messages discouraging you from contacting the company directly.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Unsolicited emails or DMs offering jobs, scholarships, or honors.
  • Offers that require you to pay to receive benefits.
  • Links to Google Forms, shortened URLs, or fake application portals.
  • Messages with grammatical errors, vague job descriptions, or sketchy logos.
  • Recruiters who avoid phone or video calls and rush the process.
  • Instructions to keep the job confidential or avoid contacting the organization.

If You Fell for a Job or Scholarship Scam

  1. Stop all communication with the scammer.
  2. Report the scam to your school, the FTC at ftc.gov/Complaint, and the platform it appeared on (LinkedIn, Instagram, email provider).
  3. Contact your bank if you sent money or deposited a fraudulent check.
  4. Change your passwords for email, LinkedIn, or any other accounts you gave access to.
  5. Place a fraud alert or credit freeze if you shared sensitive information like your SSN.
  6. Warn others: Tell your classmates, coworkers, or campus job board to prevent further victims.
