Tech Support and IT Scams

Understanding the Threat: Tech support scams trick users into believing their computers or devices are infected with viruses or compromised. Scammers may use fake pop-ups, unsolicited phone calls, or emails to impersonate technical support agents from trusted companies like Microsoft, Apple, or your internet provider. Their goal is to gain remote access to your system or pressure you into paying for unnecessary “repairs.”

Common Tactics in Tech Support Scams

  • Fake Pop-Ups with Scary Alerts: Example: A webpage freezes and displays a message like “Your system has been infected! Call Apple Support now.” (The goal is to frighten the user into calling a fake tech support number.)
  • Caller ID Spoofing: Example: A scammer calls, and your phone says it’s from “Microsoft Support.” (In reality, the number is spoofed. The caller is not affiliated with any tech company.)
  • Remote Access Installation: Example: You’re told to install software like TeamViewer, AnyDesk, or LogMeIn. (Once installed, the scammer can control your system, steal data, or plant malware.)
  • Pressure to Pay for Fake Fixes: Example: After running fake diagnostics, the scammer demands payment for virus removal, software licenses, or “support contracts.” (Payment is requested via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or direct transfer—making it harder to trace.)

How to Spot a Tech Support Scam

  • Sudden pop-ups or error messages with urgent warnings.
  • Messages claiming your personal information is at risk without verification.
  • Prompts to call a toll-free number immediately.
  • Requests to install software you’ve never heard of.
  • Callers demanding payment on the spot for repairs or cleanup.
  • Poor grammar, technical buzzwords, or robotic scripts in communications.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Pop-ups that freeze your browser or claim your device is infected.
  • Messages urging you to call a tech support number or contact “certified technicians.”
  • Webpages pretending to be antivirus or system scan results.
  • Scare tactics, such as warnings of stolen passwords or identity theft.
  • Requests to give someone remote control of your computer.
  • Payment demands in unconventional forms like gift cards or cryptocurrency.
  • Threats of legal action or account suspension tied to technical issues.

If You Fell for a Tech Support Scam

  1. Disconnect from the internet: This can halt the scammer’s remote access if software was installed.
  2. Uninstall any remote-access programs: Immediately remove apps like TeamViewer or AnyDesk if you were tricked into installing them.
  3. Run a full antivirus and malware scan: Use trusted software to remove any threats.
  4. Change your passwords: Focus on banking, email, and any accounts that may have been accessed.
  5. Contact your bank or credit card company: If you paid the scammer, dispute the charges and monitor your accounts.
  6. Report the scam: File a report with the FTC at ftc.gov/Complaint or report it to the platform involved (Google, Apple, Microsoft).
